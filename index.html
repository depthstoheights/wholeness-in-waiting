Honest Devotionals

Real talk about real struggles

When Your Bed Feels Empty

Let's be honest: sometimes the loneliness is physical. You don't just want someone to talk to; you want someone to hold you. You want skin-to-skin connection, the warmth of another body, the intimacy of being known physically.

This isn't shameful. God created you as an embodied being. Jesus himself sought physical comfort‚Äî he asked his disciples to stay awake with him in Gethsemane. He let John lean against his chest. He wept and needed comfort.

What to do: Don't spiritualize away your physical needs. Get massages. Hug friends longer. Hold babies. Get a pet. Exercise. Dance. Your body needs touch and movement. Honor that need in holy ways.

When Sexual Desire Feels Overwhelming

Some days, your sexuality feels like a tidal wave threatening to sweep you away. You wonder why God gave you such strong desires with no "legitimate" outlet. You're tired of being told to "take cold showers" or "think about something else."

Here's the truth: Your sexual desire is not a mistake. It's not a punishment. It's not something to be ashamed of. It's proof that you're alive, that you're human, that you were created for intimacy and connection.

Sexual energy is life energy. Channel it into creativity, service, physical activity, deep friendships. Let it fuel your prayers‚Äîyes, even prayers about sex. God isn't shocked by your desires. He created them. Talk to Him about them honestly.

"For we do not have a high priest who is unable to empathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are‚Äîyet he did not sin."
Hebrews 4:15

When Everyone Else Is Getting Married

Another engagement announcement. Another wedding invitation. Another baby shower. You smile, congratulate, celebrate‚Äîand then go home and cry. You wonder what's wrong with you. Why hasn't it happened for you?

Nothing is wrong with you. Your singleness is not a character flaw, a punishment, or a sign that you're unlovable. Some of the most amazing, attractive, godly people are single. Jesus was single. Paul was single. Singleness is not a consolation prize for those who couldn't "achieve" marriage.

Grieve if you need to. It's okay to mourn the life you thought you'd have by now. But don't get stuck there. Your life is not on hold. You're not in the waiting room. This is your real life, happening right now. Live it fully.

When The Church Makes You Feel "Less Than"

The church often doesn't know what to do with singles, especially older singles. You're too old for the youth group, but don't fit in the "couples class." Sermons assume everyone is married with 2.5 kids. You feel invisible, forgotten, like you don't quite belong.

Your singleness is not a problem to be solved. You are not a project. You are not incomplete. You are a full member of the body of Christ, with gifts to offer and a vital role to play. The church needs your perspective, your availability, your unique contributions.

If your church doesn't see your value, that's their loss, not your deficiency. Find ways to serve that honor your singleness. Start a singles ministry if there isn't one. Be the representation you wish you had seen.

When You're Tired of Being Strong

Sometimes you're exhausted from being independent, from handling everything alone, from being your own support system. You want someone else to be strong for you, to take care of you, to tell you it's going to be okay.

It's okay to not be okay. It's okay to admit you're struggling. Independence doesn't mean isolation. You need people. We all do. Build your chosen family. Invest deeply in friendships. Ask for help. Let people love you.

Remember:
Even Jesus needed Simon to carry his cross. Strength isn't doing everything alone; it's knowing when to lean on others and on God.

Holy Rhythms

Practical patterns for thriving

Daily Rhythms

Morning: Grounding (15 mins)
Before checking your phone or comparing your life to others on social media, ground yourself in truth. Read Scripture. Pray. Remind yourself who you are in Christ. Set your intention for the day.
Midday: Connection (5 mins)
Reach out to someone. Send an encouraging text. Make a quick call. Combat isolation by initiating connection. Don't wait for others to reach out first.
Evening: Movement (30 mins)
Move your body. Exercise, dance, walk, swim. Physical movement helps process emotional and sexual energy. It's a form of prayer, worship, and self-care.
Night: Gratitude (10 mins)
Before bed, list three things you're grateful for from today. Include at least one thing about your singleness. Train your brain to see gifts, not just gaps.

Weekly Rhythms

Sabbath Practice
Take one day to rest, play, and delight. Do things that bring you joy, not productivity. This is especially important for singles who often feel pressure to be constantly productive to prove their worth.
Deep Connection
Schedule at least one meaningful, in-person connection each week. Coffee with a friend, dinner with family, serving alongside others. Fight against the tendency to isolate.
Creative Expression
Dedicate time to creating something. Art, music, writing, cooking, building. Creation is a way to channel life energy and connect with the Creator.
Physical Care
Do something to care for your body. Get a massage, take a long bath, do yoga, get a haircut. Your body is a temple, even (especially) as a single person.

Monthly Rhythms

Adventure Day
Once a month, do something adventurous. Travel somewhere new, try a new restaurant, take a class, explore your city. Don't wait for someone else to have experiences with.
Service Project
Use your freedom to serve. Volunteer, mentor someone, use your skills for others. Your singleness gives you availability that's a gift to the world.
Retreat Time
Take a personal retreat. Even if just for a few hours. Get alone with God. Journal. Process. Listen. Your relationship with God is your primary relationship.

Rhythms for Difficult Days

When Loneliness Hits
1. Acknowledge the feeling without judgment
2. Reach out to someone (text, call, or visit)
3. Do something kind for another person
4. Move your body (walk, dance, exercise)
5. Remember: Loneliness is a feeling, not a fact
When Sexual Temptation Peaks
1. HALT check: Am I Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired?
2. Change your physical environment immediately
3. Call/text an accountability friend
4. Engage in vigorous physical activity
5. Pray honestly about what you're feeling
6. Remember your "why" for choosing purity

The Truth About Sexual Desire

Your sexual desire is not a curse, a mistake, or a punishment for being single. It's evidence that you're created in God's image‚Äîmade for intimacy, connection, and pleasure. The challenge isn't to eliminate desire but to steward it wisely.

What Scripture Actually Says

  • Sexual desire itself is not sin‚Äîit's part of being human
  • God created sex and called it "very good" (Genesis 1:31)
  • The Song of Solomon celebrates sexual desire and pleasure
  • Jesus was fully human, meaning he experienced all human desires
  • Paul acknowledges the strength of sexual desire (1 Cor 7:9)

What This Means for You

Stop shaming yourself for having desires. Instead, learn to acknowledge them, talk to God about them, and channel them in life-giving ways. Your sexuality is part of your humanity, not something to be suppressed until marriage.

Practical Strategies for Sexual Integrity

1. Understand Your Triggers
What situations, times, or emotional states make you most vulnerable? Late nights? After conflict? When you're lonely? Stressed? Bored? Know your patterns and plan accordingly. This isn't about perfection; it's about wisdom.
2. Channel Sexual Energy
Sexual energy is life energy. When it rises, channel it:
  • ‚Ä¢ Into creative projects (art, music, writing)
  • ‚Ä¢ Into physical activity (running, dancing, sports)
  • ‚Ä¢ Into service and connection with others
  • ‚Ä¢ Into passionate prayer and worship
  • ‚Ä¢ Into learning and intellectual pursuits
3. Practice Embodied Spirituality
Don't try to be a floating head Christian. You have a body. Honor it:
  • ‚Ä¢ Get regular massages for healthy touch
  • ‚Ä¢ Exercise to stay connected to your body
  • ‚Ä¢ Dance to express yourself physically
  • ‚Ä¢ Practice breathing exercises
  • ‚Ä¢ Take care of your physical appearance
4. Build Intimate (Non-Sexual) Relationships
Intimacy isn't just sexual. Build deep friendships where you're known, seen, and loved. Share your struggles. Be vulnerable. Create chosen family. Many of your intimacy needs can be met through platonic relationships.

When You Fail

Let's be real: You might fail. You might cross boundaries you set. You might fall into pornography, masturbation, or inappropriate relationships. What then?

Remember Grace
"If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us our sins and purify us from all unrighteousness" (1 John 1:9). Your failure doesn't disqualify you. Get up, receive grace, and keep going.
Learn from It
What led to the failure? What could you do differently? Every failure is data that can lead to future success. Don't waste your failures; learn from them.
Get Support
You cannot do this alone. Find someone safe to be honest with. Join a support group. Get counseling if needed. Isolation and shame will keep you stuck; community brings freedom.

A Different Perspective on Waiting

What if you're not "waiting" for marriage to experience sexual fulfillment? What if you're cultivating sexual wholeness right now? What if your journey with your sexuality as a single person is preparing you to be a better lover if/when you marry?

The self-knowledge, self-control, and deep relationship with God you're developing will serve you whether you marry or remain single. You're not on hold; you're becoming whole.

Loneliness & Connection

Building meaningful relationships

The Truth About Loneliness

First, let's be clear: Loneliness is not unique to singles. Many married people are desperately lonely. Marriage doesn't cure loneliness; connection does. And connection is available to you right now, as a single person.

Types of Loneliness

Emotional Loneliness
The absence of someone who "gets" you. Combat this by deepening existing friendships, joining groups with shared interests, being vulnerable with safe people.
Social Loneliness
Lack of a social network. Address this by joining clubs, attending church events, volunteering, saying yes to invitations even when you don't feel like it.
Spiritual Loneliness
Feeling disconnected from God. Remedy through prayer, meditation, worship, spiritual direction, and remembering that feelings aren't facts.
Physical Loneliness
Lack of physical touch and presence. Address through hugs, massage, pets, exercise classes, dance, and being around people even without interaction.

Building Your Chosen Family

As a single person, you have the opportunity to build a chosen family‚Äî deep, committed friendships that provide mutual support, celebration, and belonging.

How to Build Deep Friendships

  • Be Intentional: Don't wait for friendships to "happen." Pursue people.
  • Be Consistent: Regular contact builds connection. Schedule recurring time.
  • Be Vulnerable: Share your real self, not just your highlight reel.
  • Be Committed: Treat friendships with the commitment usually reserved for romance.
  • Be Present: Put away your phone. Listen deeply. Show up fully.
  • Be Generous: Give without keeping score. Celebrate others' wins.

Creating Connection Opportunities

Host Regular Gatherings
Weekly dinner, monthly game night, quarterly parties. Be the person who brings people together. Your home doesn't have to be perfect; it just has to be open.
Join or Start a Group
Book club, hiking group, cooking class, Bible study, volunteer team. Shared activities create natural connection points. If nothing exists, start something.
Practice Micro-Connections
Chat with the barista, smile at strangers, compliment someone, help a neighbor. Small connections throughout the day combat isolation.
Embrace Intergenerational Friendships
Don't limit yourself to people your age. Mentor younger people, learn from older people. Some of the richest friendships cross generational lines.

When Loneliness Feels Unbearable

"God sets the lonely in families, he leads out the prisoners with singing."
Psalm 68:6

Emergency Loneliness Plan

  1. Acknowledge the feeling without shame
  2. Remember that loneliness is temporary, even when it doesn't feel like it
  3. Reach out to someone‚Äîanyone. Send a text, make a call
  4. Get out of your house. Go to a coffee shop, library, gym
  5. Do something for someone else. Service breaks the spiral
  6. Talk to God honestly about how you're feeling
  7. Remember: You are not alone, even when you feel alone

The Gift of Solitude

There's a difference between loneliness and solitude. Loneliness is the pain of being alone. Solitude is the glory of being alone. As a single person, you have the opportunity to cultivate solitude‚Äî to enjoy your own company, to know yourself deeply, to commune with God without distraction.

Learn to be alone without being lonely. This is a superpower that will serve you whether you remain single or marry. The person who can be happily alone is never truly lonely.
